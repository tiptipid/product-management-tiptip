2. Strategic Framing: Explore vs. Exploit
March (1991) Organizational Learning Model Applied to SatuSatu · March 2026

March's (1991) organizational learning model distinguishes two modes of operation: exploitation — refining and extending existing knowledge, processes, and capabilities — and exploration — discovering new possibilities, testing hypotheses, and building new capabilities. Neither mode is inherently superior; the strategic question is sequencing and balance. Over-exploitation traps organizations in local optima, unable to adapt when market conditions shift. Over-exploration dissipates resources across unvalidated bets before a proven revenue foundation is established.

For SatuSatu — post-launch with domestic traction but zero proven foreign visitor acquisition — the risk runs in both directions. The platform has accumulated Explore debt (too many experimental features, insufficient UX polish on fundamentals) while simultaneously under-investing in Exploit work that would convert the foreign visitors it already receives. The current estimated ratio of 65% Explore / 35% Exploit is strategically misaligned for a platform at this stage. The recommended rebalancing to 70% Exploit / 30% Explore for the NOW and NEXT horizons reflects the primacy of funnel repair before market expansion.

Recommended Ratio & Sequencing Principle

The recommended 70/30 Exploit/Explore ratio for the NOW and NEXT horizons is not an arbitrary constraint — it reflects the empirical finding that platforms at SatuSatu's stage (early product-market fit, sub-500 monthly foreign bookings) overwhelmingly fail due to conversion leakage rather than insufficient product breadth. Klook and GetYourGuide did not win by being the most innovative platforms in 2012 — they won by being the most reliable and frictionless ones.

Sequencing principle: Exploit initiatives must gate Explore ones. Guest checkout must ship before loyalty credits are meaningful. Free cancellation badge must be accurate before it is displayed. I18n translations must be live before Korean/Hindi localization is worth investing in. Each Explore bet must have a named prerequisite Exploit initiative — if that prerequisite is not validated, the Explore bet stays in the LATER horizon regardless of ICE score.

3.2 Why ICE + Kano for SatuSatu / TipTip

(a) Cold-Start Problem. SatuSatu has no baseline data on foreign visitor behaviour — no cohort retention curves, no attributed conversion rates by nationality, no A/B test history. RICE's Reach dimension would require PM teams to fabricate numbers, producing false precision that gives stakeholders unwarranted confidence in prioritization decisions. ICE's Confidence dimension, by contrast, explicitly surfaces epistemic uncertainty: a Confidence score of 5 signals "this is a competitive analogy, not a measured fact," which is an honest and useful piece of information at the pre-data stage.

(b) Team Size & Execution Velocity. With approximately 10 engineers across PAYCOM and CONTEX squads, SatuSatu's PM team cannot afford the calibration overhead of WSJF Cost of Delay matrices or RICE multi-session scoring workshops. ICE sessions — Impact, Confidence, Ease scored 1–10 with a 3-person panel — take under 30 minutes per sprint cycle. The simplicity is a feature, not a limitation: it keeps the team in the building-and-learning loop rather than the planning-and-documenting loop.

(c) VC Accountability & Stakeholder Communication. ICE scores are one-sentence-explainable: "we scored this P1 because Impact=9 from Klook guest-checkout uplift data, Confidence=8 because it's a known pattern, and Ease=6 because SSO Phase 1 is already in testing." This defensibility is critical when CPO-level decisions are reviewed by investment committees. WSJF or RICE scores, without calibration history, look rigorous but are not — and they cannot withstand probing questions in the same way.

(d) Kano Enforcement — Preventing Delight Before Basic. The Kano classification layer serves as a hard gate that ICE alone cannot provide. A Delight feature with ICE=400 must not ship before a Basic feature with ICE=300 — because un-met Basic features cause dissatisfaction (users leave), while un-met Delight features are simply missed opportunities (users don't notice). GetYourGuide, Klook, and KKday have already established customer expectations for free cancellation, guest checkout, and upfront pricing as table-stakes basics. Until SatuSatu matches these, Delight features (loyalty, recommendations, WhatsApp booking) are being built on a foundation that leaks.

3.3 How to Run an ICE Scoring Session

Step 1: Kano Classification First. Before any ICE scoring, classify each initiative as Basic, Performance, or Delight. Basic features are automatically elevated to NOW horizon regardless of ICE score, provided Engineering Ease ≥ 4. The Kano gate must be applied before ICE multiplication.
Step 2: ICE Dimensions Definition. Impact = business outcome magnitude (1–10), anchored by competitor evidence proxies when internal data is unavailable (e.g., "Klook reported 23% checkout conversion uplift post-guest-checkout" = anchor for Impact=9). Confidence = evidence quality (1–10): published data=8–10, competitive analogy=5–7, PM hypothesis=1–4. Ease = inverse of engineering days (10=XS, 8=S, 6=M, 4=L, 2=XL). ICE = Impact × Confidence × Ease.
Step 3: Async Scoring. Product Lead, Engineering Lead, and Commercial Lead each score independently (no anchoring). Average the three scores. If any single dimension diverges by more than 3 points between scorers, the item enters a mandatory 15-minute calibration discussion before the score is finalized. This prevents groupthink without requiring consensus for every item.
Step 4: Explore/Exploit Sanity Check. After scoring, count the NOW horizon items. If more than 40% of NOW items are classified Explore, force a rebalancing discussion. The product team must explicitly name which Exploit items are being deferred and justify the trade-off to the CPO before the sprint begins.
Step 5: VC Intervention Protocol. When an initiative is mandated by investors or board with insufficient ICE evidence, label it P0-VC-Mandated and document the opportunity cost — the specific ICE-scored item(s) being displaced from the sprint. This creates accountability and a historical record for post-launch retrospectives.

7.6 Why These Metrics Matter for SatuSatu

Acquisition: SatuSatu currently has zero organic rank for "Bali activities" — every new foreign session is a hard-won asset that must be tracked to justify SEO investment and paid channel efficiency. Without foreign session segmentation, any aggregate growth metric could be entirely domestic uplift, which would not validate the foreign visitor thesis.

Evaluation/Trust: The trust deficit (score 3.5/10 vs. OTA 7/10 at Evaluation) is the single biggest leakage point identified in the UX audit. Listing trust score (composite: has badge + review count + has cancellation policy) and detail dwell time are the earliest detectable signals of trust recovery — they will move before booking numbers move, giving the team a 2–4 week leading indicator.

Conversion: The auth exit rate, estimated above 60% for foreign visitors, represents the largest single recoverable revenue pool in the NOW horizon. Each percentage point of improvement in account exit rate at the auth wall is directly translatable to a measurable increase in booking revenue. This metric alone justifies the entire guest checkout initiative.

Retention: Foreign visitors book Bali trips infrequently — annual or biannual at best. A 12% D30 return rate target is ambitious but critical: each returning user is proof of brand preference over Klook and GetYourGuide, not just price discovery. Post-experience review rate is the earliest proxy for whether the experience loop closes — a user who leaves a review is 3x more likely to book again than one who does not.

PM Review Ritual — 4 Cadences

Monday Morning · 15 Minutes — Leading Indicator Pulse
Review metrics 1 (Foreign Session Volume), 10 (Auth Wall Exit Rate), 11 (Checkout Drop-off), 13 (Foreign Booking Share), 18 (Weekly Qualified Bookings). Flag any metric that hit its alert threshold. No slide deck — verbal standup or async Notion comment. Focus: are we in trend or in trouble?
Sprint Review · Bi-Weekly — Initiative Impact Check
For each initiative shipped in the sprint, compare before/after on its primary metric (from the Roadmap Driver column). Use 7-day pre/post windows minimum. Record the delta in the initiative's Notion page. Celebrate green arrows; investigate flat lines — do not accept "it's too early to tell" for more than one sprint cycle.
Monthly · 1 Hour — North Star + Lagging Narrative
Report format: (1) "Monthly Foreign Visitor Bookings Completed: [N] — [+X%/−X%] vs. last month." (2) "Three leading indicators that will affect next month: [metric name, current value, trajectory]." (3) "One initiative shipping next month and its expected metric impact." Three sentences only — force the team to prioritise signal over noise.
Quarterly · Half Day — Metrics Audit + Retirement
Review the full 18-metric stack. Retire any metric where: (a) it has been green for 3 consecutive months (graduated to baseline), (b) the initiative it was tracking has shipped and stabilised, or (c) the tool collecting it has proven unreliable. Add new metrics only if a new initiative introduces a new causal hypothesis not covered by existing metrics.

10. Counter-Metrics: Preventing Gaming
Every primary metric paired with a counter-metric to detect perverse incentives
Why Counter-Metrics Matter: Every metric that feeds a PM's performance evaluation or a team's sprint velocity will eventually be gamed — not necessarily with malicious intent, but through unconscious optimisation toward the measured variable. Counter-metrics are not punitive; they are the second instrument in a stereo system. You need both channels to hear the full picture. For a pre-PMF platform like SatuSatu, undetected gaming can produce months of false positive signals before the funnel collapse becomes visible in lagging indicators.

11. Risk & Trade-off Analysis (Per Initiative)
Why-before-PRD framework — each initiative's assumptions, risks, and RACI before engineering begins

11.1 Why Before PRD

Why document risks before writing the PRD?
  • Unchecked assumptions in a PRD become unchecked assumptions in code — the later they surface, the more expensive the rework
  • Explicit risk documentation creates a psychological permission structure for engineers to raise concerns before sprint start, not during it
  • When an initiative fails or underperforms, the pre-documented risk register distinguishes "we knew this was a risk and accepted it" from "we didn't know" — the former is a learning, the latter is a process failure
  • VC and board reviews of product decisions are substantially more credible when the team can demonstrate that trade-offs were consciously evaluated, not discovered post-launch
